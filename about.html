About DiTTA

DiTTA (Digital Twin Technology for Aerospace Engineering) is an innovative technology project developed by Cal Poly Pomona aimed at creating advanced digital twin technology for aerospace engineering applications. The primary objective of the CPP-DiTTA project is to develop a technology demonstrator for a digital twin, which serves as a computational model of a physical asset updated with real-time sensory data. This concept is of significant interest to the aerospace community, including the US Air Force and private companies, as it offers substantial benefits in terms of maintenance cost reduction and improved decision-making through live health monitoring and predictive analysis.

The primary objective of the CPP-DiTTA project is to develop a technology demonstrator for a digital twin, which serves as a computational model of a physical asset updated with real-time sensory data. This concept is of significant interest to the aerospace community, as it offers substantial benefits in terms of maintenance cost reduction and improved decision-making through live health monitoring and predictive analysis.

The first iteration of the project, CPP-DiTTA 1.0, involved developing a digital twin of an aluminum plate. This system was capable of predicting the location of damage on the plate using real-time sensory data and producing a three-dimensional visualization of the deformed configuration under specific loads. The initial results were reported at SciTech 2024. Building on this success, CPP-DiTTA 2.0 focuses on a more complex composite plate and aims to detect internal delamination defects using real-time sensor data. The enhanced version will be showcased at SciTech 2025.

Digital twins provide a tail number-specific computational model of physical assets, continuously updated based on sensory data throughout the asset's life. This live monitoring system helps in identifying and predicting in-service damage, such as internal delamination, which can occur due to mechanical impacts and environmental conditions. The project uses advanced finite element analysis (FEA) and machine learning (ML) algorithms to detect and visualize these defects. High-fidelity simulation models are built to study various damage scenarios, which then inform the ML models used in the decision-making module of the digital twin.

The CPP-DiTTA system architecture comprises several key components: the physical asset (a composite plate in the latest version), a digital twin that updates at regular intervals based on sensory data, and a virtual reality module for intuitive visualization. The physical asset is equipped with sensors to measure stress and deformation, with data being fed into high-fidelity simulations and ML models. These models, including decision trees and neural networks, predict the location and extent of damage, providing critical insights into the asset's health.

The visualization module, developed using the Unity 3D game engine, allows users to interact with the digital twin in a virtual environment, enhancing the understanding of simulation results. This integration of real-time data, advanced simulations, and intuitive visualization makes DiTTA a powerful tool for research, education, and practical applications in aerospace engineering, offering a comprehensive solution for digital twin technology.
